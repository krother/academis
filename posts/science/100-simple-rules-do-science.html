100 Simple Rules to do Science

When you are a researcher, you usually have plenty of experiments to do and literature to read. On top of that it is expected that you take care of "softer" duties like communicating with other scientists, getting funding and planning your career. When you are new to science, this sums up to a big pile of work. With more experience it is still a big pile of work. But eventually you will figure out how to get things done more efficiently and have fun doing it.

Realizing this, Phil Bourne initiated the "Ten Simple Rules" series to help new scientists getting started. Numerous authors have contributed to more than 15 two-page articles published in PLoS Computational Biology. I think the articles give undergraduate and PhD students a realistic understanding of how science works. My ten favourite articles from the "Ten Simple Rules" series are:

Present Ten Simple Rules in a journal club

One criticism of the 'Ten Simple Rules' articles is that they leave many practical questions open. For instance, you don't learn how to write a paper from reading two pages. But they help you to avoid the biggest mistakes. In my opinion the Ten Simple Rules articles are ideal for starting a discussion on the subject. For instance, as an undergraduate you could present one of the articles during a journal club and ask more experienced scientists to share their experience. Just keep Rule #2 for presenting in mind: Less is More. Ten rules at a time are enough. After all, you have experiments to do as well.
