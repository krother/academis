Repeat and reinforce a complex system

1.5 hours

3-15

Good reasons to do a puzzle

\"Not another 30 minutes of Powerpoint presentation!\". If the audience in a tutorial session has this thought, it is time to try something different. For instance, switch from slides to a haptic medium. Solving a puzzle, students can interact, reflect and reinforce what they heard in the presentation, and possibly develop questions for a follow-up discussion.

How it was done

The exercise on transcriptional regulation (see Figures) was done as part of a one hour tutorial session in the Theoretical Biophysics Group at the HU Berlin. After a warm-up exercise and 20-25 minutes of presentation on the subject, groups of participants got a set of 17 labeled paper proteins, a big sheet of paper, and a thick pen along with a task description:

Assemble the parts of the transcription machinery given on the paper cards.
Draw the DNA that undergoes transcription including the strand direction,core promoter, TATA element, and upstream activator sequence.

The participants assembled the transcriptional machinery for about 20 minutes, followed by a summary and an open discussion.

How to get it right

If you are planning to create a puzzle with molecules (or any other complex system), there are a few points worth considering:

  • Leave degrees of freedom. It is good if there is more than one correct solution. This gives all your listeners a fair chance to get it right. In the above example, the exact position of each component was not crucial, just the arrangement of the main groups.
  • Require domain-specific knowledge. If your puzzle can be solved by a 5-year-old, the science part got lost. To fit the parts together, the key should be in the main message of your presentation (or previous knowledge).
  • The components may give clues. Shape and color could convey helpful information, for instance grouping elements together, or one piece serving as a template on which others are assembled (like the red polymerase in the figuer). 
  • Highlight key features in a summary. Explain what is important in your system in the summary. Point out what your participants did well - and skip small mistakes generously.
If you feel your puzzle is rather a little bit too easy, then you probably got it right. Your audience does not need to solve a tough chestnut. Simply digesting the subject with their own hands supports them to gain an overview over a complex topic, and remember.
