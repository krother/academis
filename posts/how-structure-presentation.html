How to structure a Presentation?

Many scientists assume that the default structure of a scientific presentation should be:

  1. Introduction

  2. Methods

  3. Results

  4. Discussion

You even find detailed instructions on how many slides each section should have. This four-part structure has the advantage that it is easy for the presenter to implement. However, I think it neglects three key areas contributing to a successful presentation:

  • Intelligibility
    A presentation is not a paper. The structure above is useful for a paper because it helps you to find the according parts of an experiment quickly. However, you cannot jump back and forth in a live presentation. If you missed out a detail when the speaker described his method ten minutes earlier, you are lost.

  • Purpose
    The purpose of a manuscript is usually to report an experiment. With a presentation this is not necessarily the case. It could be as well to persuade the audience of a theory or simply get yourself a job. The structure of the presentation needs to contribute to your goal.

  • Variety
    When you have to listen to 10+ talks all following the same structure, you might get bored more easily. Of course it depends on the topic, but I guess I am not the only person suffering from conference narcolepsy.

Alternative structures

A more flexible structure consists of an opening, a body with 3-5 main points, and a closing. The main purpose of the opening is to gain the attention of your audience. The body can be implemented in several ways:

Enumeration
1. First example; 2. Second example; 3. Third example
Timeline
1. Past; 2. Present; 3. Future
Comparison
1. First hypothesis; 2. Second hypothesis; 3. Second hypothesis is better
Dramatic Structure
1. Problem; 2. Struggle; 3. Climax

When closing a presentation you can refer to your opening. This helps you to reinforce your main message. For instance, in this article I wanted to emphasize that there is more than one possible structure for a scientific talk. I hope it helps you to make a conscious choice which structure to use yourself.
